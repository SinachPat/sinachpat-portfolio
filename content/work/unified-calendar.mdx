## 01 — Context

Acme Corp is a B2B SaaS platform that helps mid-market companies manage their operational workflows. When I joined the onboarding project, the product had been live for 18 months. Growth was healthy — the marketing team was bringing in 2,400+ signups per month — but something was wrong downstream.

Over 60% of those signups never reached what we internally called the "aha moment": the point where a user creates their first automated workflow and sees it run successfully. Instead, they'd sign up, poke around, and quietly churn.

> The pipeline was full, but the bucket was leaking. We weren't losing users to competitors — we were losing them to confusion.

---

## 02 — The Problem

The existing onboarding flow had 14 screens. Fourteen. It collected company size, industry, budget range, team count, and role — all before the user had seen a single feature. The flow had been designed by committee over 18 months, with every team wanting their own data collected upfront.

When I audited the funnel, the pattern was stark:

1. Step 1 (email/password) — 100% completion
2. Step 2 (company info) — 74% completion
3. Step 3 (team size + budget) — 41% completion
4. Steps 4–14 — effectively dead; fewer than 28% reached the dashboard

The drop-off at step 3 was the cliff. We were asking for commitment before we'd demonstrated any value.

---

## 03 — Research

I ran 12 moderated user interviews across three segments: solo operators, team leads, and enterprise evaluators. I also analyzed 400 session recordings from FullStory and reviewed 6 months of onboarding-related support tickets.

Key findings:

- **Users didn't understand the value proposition from the marketing site.** They knew Acme "automated workflows" but couldn't articulate what that meant for their daily work.
- **The signup form felt like a procurement form.** Three participants compared it to filling out a vendor assessment. One said: "I just wanted to try it, not commit to a contract."
- **The most valuable feature — the visual workflow builder — was buried 6 clicks deep.** Users who found it organically had 3x higher retention, but most never got there.
- **Support tickets were dominated by "how do I start?" questions.** 67% of tickets in the first 7 days were orientation-related, not bug-related.

---

## 04 — Framing the Solution

Based on the research, I framed the redesign around three principles:

1. **Value before commitment.** Don't ask for anything until you've shown something worth caring about.
2. **Action over explanation.** Show the product, don't explain it. Let users do something meaningful in the first session.
3. **Progressive disclosure.** Collect data only when it becomes relevant to the user's experience — not when it's convenient for our internal teams.

I proposed eliminating the entire information-gathering phase from onboarding and replacing it with what I called a "value-first" activation model.

> The hypothesis was simple: if we could get users to build and run one workflow in their first session, everything else — data collection, team invites, upgrade conversations — would follow naturally.

---

## 05 — The Design

The redesigned onboarding has three phases:

### Phase 1: Frictionless Entry
Three steps to reach the product: email, verify, choose a workspace name. No company size. No budget. No role selection. Total time: under 60 seconds.

### Phase 2: Guided First Action
Instead of a blank dashboard, new users land on an interactive walkthrough. They're presented with three pre-built workflow templates matched to common use cases:

- **"Automate a client intake form"** — for agencies and service businesses
- **"Set up a team standup report"** — for team leads
- **"Create an approval chain"** — for operations

The user picks one and walks through customizing it with their own data. The template is functional — by the end of the walkthrough, they've built and triggered a real workflow. This takes about 4 minutes.

### Phase 3: Progressive Data Collection
After the first workflow runs, we surface a lightweight "personalize your experience" prompt. This collects company size and role — the same data we used to demand upfront — but now the user has context for why it matters. Completion rate for this step: 89%, compared to 41% for the old step 3.

---

## 06 — Execution

The project ran for 12 weeks across two sprints:

- **Weeks 1–4:** Research, framing, and design. I led the design process with one product designer, creating flows in Figma and testing with 8 prototype participants.
- **Weeks 5–10:** Engineering build. I worked daily with two frontend engineers and one backend engineer to implement the new flow, with weekly check-ins with the data team to ensure analytics coverage.
- **Weeks 11–12:** Staged rollout. We ran a 50/50 split test between old and new onboarding for 2 weeks, measuring activation rate, time-to-value, and 7-day retention.

The biggest engineering challenge was the template system. Each template needed to be both instructional (guiding the user) and functional (producing a real workflow). We solved this by building a "guided mode" layer on top of the existing workflow editor — it highlights the next step without restricting the user from exploring.

---

## 07 — Outcomes

Within 30 days of full rollout:

- **Activation rate: 22% → 61%.** Nearly 3x more users reached the "aha moment" of running their first workflow.
- **Time-to-value: 14 days → 3 days.** The median time from signup to first workflow dropped by 79%.
- **Onboarding support tickets: 400/mo → 130/mo.** A 67% reduction, freeing up the support team to focus on retention and expansion.
- **7-day retention: 34% → 52%.** Users who completed the guided walkthrough were significantly more likely to return.

The revenue impact was indirect but meaningful: by Q3 2024, the sales team reported that trial-to-paid conversion improved by 18%, which they attributed to users arriving at sales conversations with a much stronger understanding of the product.

---

## 08 — What I Learned

**Organizational alignment is harder than design.** The original 14-step onboarding existed because every team had lobbied for their data point. Removing those steps required buy-in from marketing, sales, and customer success. I spent as much time on internal alignment as I did on user research.

**Templates are a product, not a shortcut.** Building templates that are both educational and functional required careful content design. A bad template teaches nothing; a great template creates an "I could build this" moment.

**Metrics need context.** The old onboarding had high completion rates for step 1 — but that's meaningless if the overall funnel bleeds out by step 3. I restructured our dashboard to track activation rate (reaching the aha moment) rather than onboarding completion (reaching the dashboard).

---

## 09 — Reflection

If I were to revisit this project, I'd invest more in the post-activation experience. We nailed the first session, but the transition from "guided" to "self-directed" still has friction. Users complete the walkthrough but sometimes struggle to create their second workflow independently.

I'd also explore personalized template recommendations based on signup source. Users coming from a blog post about team management have different needs than those arriving from a Google ad about automation — and the template selection screen could be smarter about this.

> The core insight from this project: people need to understand value before they're willing to invest effort. Every form field you show before the "aha moment" is a tax on curiosity.
